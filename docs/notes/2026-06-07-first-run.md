### First-proposal bias observation (updated 2026-06-08 after response-order analysis)

The original note stated this was "consistent with the first-proposal bias described in Bansal et al. (2025)." That claim was premature. After querying Postgres response-order data across 5 runs, the picture is more nuanced:

**Response-order data for baseline_v1 (Jackson Miller):**  
Proposals received in DB insertion order: Sol y Sazón $8.66 (first) → Casa de Sabor $8.31 → Margarita Mesa $11.30  
Jackson paid Sol y Sazón — the **first proposer**. This instance IS consistent with first-proposal bias.

**Across 5 runs, Jackson Miller's first-proposer win rate: 4/5 (80%)**  
This is above the naive chance rate (~50% with 2 proposals, ~33% with 3), supporting a weak first-proposal bias for this customer.

**However:**  
- In baseline_v3, Jackson received proposals in order $8.31 → $8.66 → $11.30 (optimal first), yet paid $8.66 (the second proposal). This is the **opposite** of first-proposal bias — the customer had the optimal option available first and still chose worse. This suggests LLM decision-making noise, not a pure first-proposal mechanism.
- Susan Young's first-proposer win rate: 1/5 (20%) — she consistently waits and selects cheapest.
- Angela Ng's first-proposer win rate: 1/5 (20%) — same pattern.

**Aggregate across all 15 customer-run trials: 6/15 (40%)** first-proposer wins.

**Honest characterisation:**  
Jackson Miller exhibits above-chance first-proposer selection (80%), driven by his single-item request making him less discriminating — he tends to accept the first adequate proposal. Susan and Angela (multi-item requests) actively wait for the cheapest. The observation is better described as **single-item demand making customers more susceptible to accepting early proposals** than as a replication of the Bansal et al. speed-over-quality bias. The Bansal et al. bias is about Service agents strategically over-promising response speed; what we observe is Customer agent bounded rationality. These are related but distinct mechanisms. Do not conflate them in the report without further analysis on larger scenarios.
